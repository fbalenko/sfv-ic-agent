# IC Memo Template

Output in this exact order. Use the exact section headers. Keep total length to 5–7 pages of dense markdown.

---

# {Company Name} — IC Memo

**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Analyst:** sfv-ic-agent
**Stage / Round:** {seed | Series A | Series B | etc., or "Unknown"}
**Ask:** {round size and post-money, or "Unknown"}

## 1. Recommendation

One line. **PASS / WATCH / PURSUE / TERM SHEET.** Confidence: H/M/L. One-sentence rationale.

## 2. Thesis fit

Which AI-native financial workflow is being replaced? Which SFV thesis subsegment does this map to? Why now — what changed in the last 18 months that makes this possible? Avoid generic "why now" framing (LLMs got better, regulation clarified). Be specific to this workflow and this buyer.

## 3. Product

Plain-language description of what the product does. Then the binary call:

**AI core or cosmetic?** Commit to one. If you remove the AI, does the product still function? If yes, AI is cosmetic — say so and explain. If no, AI is core — explain what specifically the AI is doing that a deterministic system could not.

## 4. Defensibility

Pick the *real* moat — do not list all four. Options: model advantage, proprietary data loop, distribution lock-in, or workflow lock-in. Why does this moat compound over time? What would a well-funded competitor need 18 months to replicate?

## 5. Market

**Lead with budget displacement, not bottom-up multiplication.** Where does this revenue come from in the buyer's existing P&L? Which line item shrinks when this product is purchased? Whose budget owns it (CFO, CIO, COO, line-of-business head)? Is that budget actually moving toward AI-native solutions, or is it stuck in renewal cycles?

Only after the budget-displacement framing, do a sanity-check sizing pass. If you write `accounts × ACV`, you are doing top-down, not bottom-up — say so honestly.

What is the realistic 5-year revenue ceiling? What would have to be true to hit it?

## 6. Team

Lead with **founder-workflow fit**: has the founding team actually done the job they are now selling software for? Be specific — name the prior role, the company, the years. Then cover relevant hires, advisors, and gaps. Note any departures from key roles since founding (this is a signal worth flagging explicitly).

## 7. Competition

Three buckets: AI-native entrants, incumbents bolting on AI, and in-house builds at the buyer. Name names in each bucket. Where does this company actually win, and against whom does it lose?

## 8. Traction

ARR, growth rate, NDR, logo quality, pipeline. **If a number is not directly cited from a source, write "Not disclosed." Do not infer from round size, do not estimate from headcount, do not benchmark to "top-decile Series A."** The only acceptable inputs are: directly cited disclosed numbers, named customer logos, public award/conference recognition, and dated press coverage.

Note any data points that are unusually strong or unusually weak — but only if those data points are themselves cited.

## 9. Top 3 risks, ranked

Ranked from most to least concerning. For each risk: what would have to be true for the risk to materialize, and what would kill it. Be specific. Generic risks ("execution risk") do not count.

## 10. Diligence plan

What would you do in week one of diligence? Specific customer references to call, specific CTO questions to ask, specific open data points to close. End with three open items the partner should weigh in on.

## 11. Rubric scoring

Score the company on the six rubric dimensions. Include a one-sentence rationale for each score. Total out of 30. Append a one-line market map entry at the bottom in the rubric format.

## 12. Sources

Numbered list of every source cited in the memo. Each entry includes: source title, URL, accessed date. Format:
[1] {Source title}. {URL}. Accessed: 2026-05-02.
[2] {Source title}. {URL}. Accessed: 2026-05-02. If you have fewer than 8 sources, you have not done enough research — go back and find more.
